A6 Part B- Molding & Casting - Fox Pattern Weight

Prompt Create a 2-part silicon mold to used to cast other materials such as plaster, soap, wax, ice, or more. The mold must be created in Rhino and then 3D printed as a negative such that silicon can be poured into the form to create the final mold. Use this silicon mold to cast at least 4 identical parts.

Looking for lamps? I've combined all lamp reports into A6 part A.

Inspiration & Idea I have done work with soap before and wanted to create a soap mold, but all my ideas only required a one-part mold.
Instead I turned my attention to some cute paperweights I could use for weighing down paper pattern pieces on my fabric for when I sew. I wanted something round and cute, so I settled on this fox from Thingiverse. (But I also have a collection of other items that might be fun to cast later.)

Fox as seen from the Thingiverse page

Creating a seam line To minimize post-processing, I wanted the seam to fall somewhere natural on the model, which meant I couldn't split it right down the middle. Instead, I wanted the seam to go down the side but follow the flow of the fox's body, meaning around its hips and around its tail. This created for a very interesting shape.
I did this by using the polyline tool to roughly draw the seam on one side of the fox, and then on the other side. I then turned on the Record History feature in Rhino and lofted the two lines so they sliced through the fox. I used the history feature to allow me to move the curves around and have the loft follow suit so I could make sure I was slicing through the correct parts in real time.
After some troubleshooting with the split feature, I turned the mesh of the fox into a polysurface by converting it from Mesh to Nurbs and then split it with my surface I created. Prior to converting to a polysurface, the geometry seemed too tough and my split function kept failing.

Slicing the fox with a plane from a pair of lofted lines I drew

Updating the model At first glance I thought the model was mostly smooth, but after bringing it into Rhino, I noticed it had more features (like eyes and a nose) that I didn't want. So before I split it, I reduced the mesh of it multiple times to try and force some of the fidelity out. I then used quad remesh to make it smoother, as I found with my A5 mesh assignment.

Designing the mold (was a nightmare) Now that I had the fox split into 2 pieces, I needed to create the mold-negative (is that the word?) But I really struggled to wrap my head around how that would look. Danli posted a diagram for us in the class chat about the process, and I referred back to it frequently throughout this assignment.

Thank you Danli. First I needed to create a ribbon offset of each half of the fox. However, I found the ribbon offset tool didn't work well with the seam being in 3D, so I attempted to make my own ribbon offset by duplicating the face edge, scaling it in 1D, manipulating the points independently, and then lofting that line to try and create the desired edge shape. Several hours later, though, I was in mesh hell and was ready to give up on my squiggly seam and just do something basic.
Some of many different iterations of creating a loft for the fox's ribbon offset Luckily I was able to connect with Junchao during office hours, who approached the problem the exact same way I did at first (that must mean I'm doing something right!) but after much hullaballoo, he met the same fate.
As we were reviewing the original model again, the idea dawned on him: we already had this surface slicing the pieces apart--why don't we just use that as the extended surface, rather than create a new offset? Genius!
From there, we stretched the plane wider and ensured it encompassed all pieces of the fox. Previously, the plane would clip the tail if extended, but Junchao showed me that I could turn points on for a surface and use those points to drag the surface around, which fixed the clipping issue. With the plane stretched wide, we then put a box around the fox (like Dr. Seuss haha) and sliced the box, removing the sliced edge of the fox and leaving only the front and back pieces. This was a piece I struggled with initially because I thought for Cura to print, the object needed to be fully attached to everything.
Using the X-Ray view on all my screens to see inside the box. Once in 2 pieces, I opted for a multipipe fitting across the edge of my mold. While most classmates are doing notches or spheres, I've had trouble using simple keys like that in the past, and with the jiggly nature of silicon, I wanted something that spanned the length of my mold rather than just a few notches. (However Junchao still recommended I use at least one sphere, which I think will help with orientation, so no harm.)
Fox in a box! Peep the multipipe. Finally, my mold-negative was done! Or was it? I ended office hours with Junchao pleased with my mold, but then I was confused again--the object I was looking at was what the silicon mold would look like... not the negative of it. I needed to invert it. After much stressing I asked Junchao--the obvious answer was in plain sight: just delete the faces that are closing it in. (And then I also had to make new faces around it on the other side so it would sit flat in the printer.)
How can everything be so obvious yet so confusing at the same time? I love 3D fabrication--why can't I think in 3D?
First: bad! Fox is the wrong way. Second: good! Fox is the right way.

Printing the mold negative One of my classmates (I forget who, sorry!) posted in the chat about scaling her print piece down in Cura for a test run, so I wanted to do that to save time to insure my 2D brain was processing this 3D piece right. I also didn't realize you could scale things in Cura! So this was a great opportunity.
When I loaded in my mold-negative, on full size (~100mm x ~100mm) at highest quality, the print was going to take 3 days--yikes! Even after dropping quality settings and infills, I was looking at an over 24hr print job. However, I scaled it down to 35% in Cura, on low quality, with 8% infill, I was looking at about 2 hours of print time, which is more than fine since I'd be running it over night.
I levelled the bed and ran the print! (Side note, I see a lot of stress about bed levelling in the class chat. I level my bed essentially before every single print and I have had no issues.)
I very much prefer taking the extra time to run my prints in the highest fidelity, but knowing this is just a test run, I had no choice but to be satisfied with the outcome. My tiny mold printed out as expected.

Cura and the mini print.

With the small print handy, I understood a little better about what pieces of the mold-negative I could cut off to reduce plastic, so I spent some time trimming down the pieces. However, the full print was still much too large and I ended up resizing it down to 70% in Cura. To speed up time and limit material (it was estimating about 200g of PLA!) I changed the infill to 8%, down from 20% since I had so much negative area due to the curvy model. I felt comfortable doing this since I knew this piece would barely be holding any weight.
After moving to printing, I was expecting a 24 hour print, but the pieces kept failing after a few hours. After examining the couple failed pieces, I went back to Cura to notice a few things.

  1. Something was going on with the way Cura decided the pieces should be printed. I noticed one piece had a red bottom and the other had a blue, indicating that one was touching the build plate but the other wasn't. At first I thought it was an issue with how I exported them, so I went back to Rhino and made sure they both sat on the same Z plane. But Cura didn't seem to notice the fix. I then stepped through the print bit by bit and noticed Cura insisted on printing part of the first half before it started on the first layers of the second half. It didn't do this on my little print. Rather than debugging why it did that, I instead decided to export them one by one and do them individually, which fixed the problem. (I also helped Courtney with hers and I noticed hers was doing the same thing in Cura. Our prints were about the same size.)
  2. While stepping through the print, I noticed the inside of my print was trying to create layers in the air and supports weren't turned on automatically. I was struggling with the triple-negative concept of the mold, so I opted to turn on supports and figure out how it would impact the model later.

Spaghetti, anyone?
Enhance. Enhance.

Mold Making with Oomoo Oomoo was fun to play with. I used Oomoo 30 (not food safe) that I ordered from Amazon. I mixed up the A and B in a 1:1 ratio by volume as instructed by the box and let it cure in my mold-negatives overnight. I had a tiny bit extra so I also poured into my mini mold-negative.

Unfortunately, pulling the silicone out didn't go as desired. I first started with the mini mold to see what would happen. I did have some concern about the funky curvy shape of my mold-negative, especially after turning on inner supports as well. My concerns became reality as I gently tugged on my mini mold and the pieces down and out of sight refused to come out. I tried prying with a knife, a toothpick, and a chopstick, and it wouldn't budge. Eventually when I thought I had it, it ripped.

Mini mold

I ran into the same issue with my big mold. Eventually, I decided that since the silicone mold was supposed to be the reusable part and not my printed mold negatives, that I would crack them open to rescue the stuck silicone. This was surprisingly more challenging than expected--thank goodness I only printed at 8% thickness!
I used a coping saw I had on hand as well as the little snippies that came with my printer. With it open, I was also able to see that the inner supports caused a tiny hole in the silicone at the tip of the fox's tail.

Tools of the trade Unfortunately I did rip one part of the mold, and I figured it wouldn't be too hard to fix. But I tried hot glue, masking tape, and electrical tape (water proof) before searching online to find that fixing a silicone mold involved more specialized chemicals that weren't worth it for this process. The rip in my mold was near the very top so I could gravity fit the broken piece and then hold everything together with pressure. #learning #growth mindset

Casting I sandwiched my two halves together and then wedged the broken silicon bit into place. I used cardboard to hold it tight and then I masking-taped it together. Boy did that fail.
I mixed my hydrostone poorly and it was too wet, but I think even in the chunkiest consistency, my mold would have leaked. After dumping, remixing, and trying again, I finally got my mold tight enough. Due to the curvy nature of my mold, I needed up and down force as well as from the sides, so I put cardboard on 2 sides and the top and bottom, with a hole cut on top for pouring. I then secured it with several rubberbands.

After 2 successful hydrostone foxes with funky tails, I tried experimenting with putting things into the hydrostone. For my first attempt, I had a bunch of left over acrylic mirror shards from my lamp, so I dumped those in hoping that some would make their way to the surface. Not a single one did, but it helped me use less hydrostone, so that's a plus. For my final fox (since I was at the end of my hydrostone) I opted for glitter, which turned out about as expected.

Final Design

My skulk of foxes! I might try creating candles next, but I'll have to stick the wick out the "bottom" of the mold since I pour into the fox from its base rather than its head. However, I think it won't be too messy compared to hydrostone because wax cures almost immediately. And to cure it even faster, I could put the mold in the freezer first. A project for another day!
