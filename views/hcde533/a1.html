A1 - Modeling & Laser Cutting - Cardboard Centipede

Prompt Make a kit consisting of at least 30 instances of at least one unique part. The parts must have slots that press fit into the other parts in the kit and are laser cut out of cardboard. The fit should be such that the parts do not come apart with a light shaking. Prior to printing, the parts must be modeled in 3D in Rhino.

Inspiration I originally considered repeated shapes in plants, but couldn't think of a quick way to prototype just one or two interlocking, repeatable pieces to create a plant out of. Then I turned my attention to bugs, that seem to have pieces that interlock together with their exoskeletons. For repeatability, I settled on a centipede.
With its body segments and angular legs, it would be a great way to met the project goal.

Sketching & Paper Prototype I sketched up a rough design of what pieces I wanted to model, settling on a single piece that would repeat to build the body and another piece that formed a pair of legs. I also considered creating antennae and a more formal head shape, but decided to put that on hold until I got to Rhino.
[images here]

Hand-cut Cardboard Prototype After paper I moved into cardboard to measure out how large the body segments should be and how the cardboard would hook together. I sketched out roughly the size I wanted and then measured it into an easier to maintain value (30cm by 50cm ellipse.) I also tried out different depths of cuts for the slots, mostly between 12 and 14mm deep. My biggest blocker was realizing that the pieces slotted into eachother, meaning I needed to account for the depth on both sides.
[images here]

Modeling in Rhino I began mocking up the pieces in Rhino based on the measurements I decided on. I'm particularly a fan of the mirror tool that allowed me to duplicate objects over themselves for faster designing. I created a 3D model of 4mm thick pieces (the thickness of the cardboard I would be cutting). I then duplicated them to see how they'd look put together. I decided for simplicity only to create the body segments and legs.

Rhino model file

Laser Cutting I exported the surfaces from my Rhino file into Inkscape to cut on the Epilog laser cutter available to me at Microsoft's makerspace, The Garage. I test cut a couple of pieces while trying to find the right settings. In the end I settled on 10% Speed and 85% Power for the laser.

After finding the settings I loaded up several pieces in the same file and proceeded to cut them close together to minimize waste. I found out midway that the cardboard I was using, due to its lossy papper backing, was causing my cuts to burn and not cut all the way through. After the first runthrough, I spent time peeling the paper off the back of the cardboard and ran the print again. This time the pieces came out mostly clean with limited burning.

Final Product After cutting I put the pieces together and ta-da! My cardboard centipede was born!
